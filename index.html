In Silicon Valley, Chinese elderly reconsider their migratory lifestyle

Although living in "the land of the free," Chinese elderly immigrants don't feel as much freedom as they do in China.

By Junyao Yang
April 27, 2023

Bingxian Wang, 60, visited the United States for the first time in 2012. His son has come to the U.S. to study the previous year and this is the first time Bingxian visited him.

Since then, he flew to the U.S. almost every year to visit his son, bringing him walnuts and pastries from his hometown in Zhejiang. The pandemic put those regular visits to a pause.

Last Christmas, he arrived again at SFO international airport. That was his first visit since the pandemic. This time, he and his wife stayed till this March, longer than any of the visits before, giving themselves the time to test out retirement life in the U.S.

—

As more young Chinese tech workers settled down in Silicon Valley, Chinese elders are also flying into the U.S. to stay with their adult children who now have their own families in the Bay Area. They come to reunite with family, help with house chores and share the childcare responsibility for their grandchildren.

For years, these older Chinese grandparents have been living a “migratory lifestyle,” as described by anthropologist Nicole Newendorp at Harvard University, traveling back and forth to balance the commitments on both sides. But just like Wang, Chinese elders who are used to the frequent traveling are now starting to reconsider this “migratory lifestyle” as the pandemic recedes.

Since 2020, China has placed severe travel restrictions, limiting the number of flights in and out of China. Direct flights between the United States and mainland China are extremely hard to come by.

Anyone entering China from overseas faces mandatory quarantines in a hotel for 14 days and more at their own expense, a requirement that wasn’t scrapped until this January.

Thus, travel between the two countries has become a huge hassle that’s costly and complicated, especially for elders who already find international travel hard to navigate.

“That really has become a time when people have to make a choice, do I actually go and stay because I’m not going to be able to go back and forth?” said Newendorp, author of the book Chinese Senior Migrants and the Globalization of Retirement published in 2020.

Some continue flying across the Pacific while others have changed their mind and decided to consider the U.S. their new home. For the latter, leaving everything behind and migrating to a new country at a later stage in life isn’t easy, especially in a country where they don’t speak the language nor understand much about the cultural and social norms.

They face language barriers when conducting the simplest daily tasks that they can easily do in China, including driving to the grocery store, running errands at the bank or just greeting their neighbor.

Another challenge is navigating the complicated intergenerational relationship between seniors and their family members. For example, they try to achieve the balance of helping out their children but not interfering with their life, or asking them for help but not being considered a burden.

At the same time, Chinese senior immigrants face the need to build new relationships outside of the family. Many seniors already have a close-knit friends circle back in China, but now they need to build new community ties that contribute to their well-being in the United States.

—

Raised under the cultural belief of filial piety, most East Asians are expected to provide care and financial support to their parents when they retire. But when aging parents and their adult children live in different countries across time zones, providing that care becomes nearly impossible, especially during the pandemic.

Summer, 37, said her parents never planned much for their retirement. They never expected how much their life quality would decline when they grow older and became sick. In 2021, Summer’s mom, who has always been in good health, accidentally fell at home and had to go through surgery that eventually led to serious complications. Soon after that, her dad was also hospitalized due to heart failure.

Summer, eight-month pregnant and living in San Francisco at the time, spent 8 hours of her day at the most on the phone with her mom, the doctors and nurses in a city near Shanghai. She couldn’t travel back herself because of her pregnancy, and all she could do was provide care and emotional support thousands of miles away.

Seeing her parents’ health deteriorating but unable to be by their side, it was an excruciating period of Summer’s life. “I feel like a hamster on a running wheel,” Summer said. “No matter how hard I try, I am still in the exact same place.”

She wondered, if she had moved her parents to San Francisco the year before, will everything be different?

—

Unlike Summer whose plan to help her parents move to the U.S. was stalled by the pandemic, many young Chinese immigrants already took the first step to move their aging parents to the U.S.

Some believe America has world-class medical experts and would be able to provide better care to their aging parents when needed, despite obstacles to finding reliable and affordable healthcare plans, especially for seniors who have pre-existing health conditions. Others simply think it helps to be living close to each other as their older parents age.

As a generation who didn’t have access to foreign language education when they were in school (if they went to school at all), most seniors don’t speak English at all.

Mingshan Liu, from Ma’an Shan, a city just about 30 miles from Nanking, never had the chance to learn much English when he was in school. He grew up around the Ma’anshan Iron & Steel Company, and in 1978, he attended the factory-run high school, where workers take the classroom and teach what they know to students. It was only the previous year when China resumed Gaokao after the Cultural Revolution, so there weren’t really many English teachers, Liu said.

Liu arrived in the Bay Area last December and has since stayed with his son and daughter-in-law in their Silicon Valley home, where the young couple used “fancy kitchen appliances,” Liu described. “I don’t understand the English words on those so I couldn’t really operate them well.”

Liu plans to start participating in English as a Second Language programs in CITY TK. “Right now I’m only in my 60s, I can still go to school and learn,” Liu said. “Just English for daily life is good enough.”

—

With their children busy with work during the workdays, Chinese seniors also face the need for a social life outside of their family members. More than 585,000 people living in the Bay Area speak Chinese at home from 2017 to 2021, according to the American Community Survey estimates, but even without language barriers, genuine connections don’t always come naturally.

Mr. Liang, an active person who loves jogging, cycling and winter swimming, talked fondly about his retirement life with a group of close friends in China. He moved to Campbell last December, where his daughter and her newborn baby live.

He misses winter swimming with his friends in Zhejiang Province, the hesitation before submerging himself in the freezing water and the exhilaration after, feeling all the blood in his body flowing to the heart. But winter swimming is not a solo sport, Mr. Liang said. His group of friends always looks out for each other.

“When swimming in the lake in the winter, someone has to break the ice for you,” he explained. “Otherwise the ice scratches your skin and there will be scars all over.”

Now, his day starts around 8 a.m. at his daughter’s house. He spent the morning looking after his grandchild and in the afternoon he sometimes walk a couple of miles to the nearby park for a jog.

“The friends back home, we all grew up in the 1970s after the Cultural Revolution. We have a common language,” Liang said. “Here, you throw away all your past and start all over.”

—

Many new Chinese elder immigrants, unlike those coming to the Bay Area earlier to join their relatives near the Chinatowns in San Francisco and Oakland, live with their adult children in suburban homes near Silicon Valley. Although these are more middle-class neighborhoods, a lack of reliable public transportation makes it difficult to get around and socialize with other people.

“The more you have an area like that, the less connected it is with public transportation to areas that might have centers where older people would find other individuals to spend time with, making it harder to find places to have those physical connections,” said Newendorp.

Although there are BART and buses available in some areas, taking public transit seems intimidating due to their limited language proficiency, and many feel unsafe riding public transportation alone. Most of the seniors don’t drive, so they rely on their children to drive them around. When their children are busy with work on workdays, they may choose not to travel anywhere for fear of becoming a burden for their kids.

The location where someone ends up moving and the possibility for transportation is one of the things that “may make the biggest difference in terms of crafting a sense of community,” Newendorp explained.

Some of her interviewees in the Boston area spend two hours on a bus each day to travel from their suburban home to Boston’s Chinatown to spend the day doing volunteer work, hoping to grasp a sense of community.

The same happens in the Bay Area. Hong Lok Senior Centers, a senior program in Oakland’s Chinatown that serve more than 300 seniors, has seen clients traveling from Fremont to Oakland, just to spend some time at the center playing chess with friends or taking on volunteer work, according to Helen Lim, the program manager who has worked there for more than 20 years.

—

As Chinese seniors navigate their transition to American life, most stay with their grown children before they could afford to rent or buy their own place. Sharing space with multiple family members, however, brings about tensions, a collision of values, and burnout for both the elderly and the younger generations. Many elders feel a strong sense of “ji-ren-li-xia” (寄人篱下) or living under someone else’s roof, a Chinese cultural concept that carries a sense of dependence, indebtedness and obligation.

Mrs. Ma, a 60-year-old woman from Beijing, has been living with her daughter and son-in-law since May 2022. Her room is right next to the bathroom, with a thin wall in between. She likes to go to bed around 10 p.m., but her son-in-law usually takes his shower at the same time. Many times she would lie in bed, she said, listening to the water splashing again the bathroom floor, and unable to sleep.

“I don’t have a home here,” Ma said. “I can’t treat someone else’s home as my own home.”

Chinese elder immigrants often contribute to their grandchildren’s upbringing by providing labor and support, but once their grandchildren grow older and go to school, they may suddenly find themselves without a sense of purpose.

“If they end up coming to help raise the grandchildren, once the grandchildren are in school, they are lost, they no longer feel needed and they don’t have a place to go,” said Heying Zhan, associate professor at the Department of Sociology, Georgia State University who published a study on the process of settlement of Chinese elder immigrants in 2019.

—

When speaking with Chinese senior migrants about their lives in the U.S. and back home, many bring up the idea of freedom (“zi-you” 自由). However, for different people, freedom could mean very different things.

While younger Chinese immigrants may feel life in the U.S. gives them the freedom to escape from the stricter political control in China, older Chinese people are more likely to see freedom as independence from others, being mobile and doing things on their own. They don’t necessarily feel as much freedom in the U.S. as they do in China, where they can go on road trips with friends, take classes in senior universities and most importantly, take care of themselves without the help from their adult children.

“People had a spectrum of ideas around what they wanted out of being here, why it was meaningful for them to be here,” said Newendorp. “But this idea of independence, I think, is one that was important.”

This April, Ma traveled back home to Beijing. During her time in the U.S., she tried to take on a lot of the responsibilities to help her daughter with childcare, so much so that she felt burned out from taking care of everything. But after nearly a year of staying in the U.S., she still finds retirement life back home much more liberating.

“Of course I have more freedom in China,” Ma said. “In the U.S. I’m mostly living for them. But in China, I’m only living for myself.”
